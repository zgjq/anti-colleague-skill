# Psychological Biases That Make Colleague-Skill Feel Convincing

## ELIZA Effect (1966)
People attribute understanding to systems that merely pattern-match surface features. The original ELIZA program was a simple regex-based therapist simulator — people still confided in it. Colleague-skill works the same way: mimic tone + vocabulary = "feels like them."

## Barnum / Forer Effect
Vague personality descriptions feel accurate because they apply to almost everyone. "认真负责，偶尔拖延" describes 90% of office workers. The "5-layer personality" structure gives a veneer of scientific rigor to horoscope-level content.

## Availability Heuristic
Chat logs are *available* data, so they feel representative. But the most important knowledge a person has is rarely written down — it's tacit, embodied, and contextual. What's easy to collect is not what's valuable.

## Illusion of Explanatory Depth
People think they understand how someone works because they've seen their messages. But predicting a person's behavior in novel situations requires understanding their *reasoning process*, not their *output format*.

## Anthropomorphism
Giving an AI a person's name and communication style triggers social brain circuits. People start treating it like a real colleague, lowering their skepticism threshold.

## Sunk Cost Fallacy (for adopters)
Once someone invests time collecting chat logs, configuring the tool, and generating a skill, they're motivated to believe it works — otherwise that time was wasted. This drives post-hoc rationalization.

## Goodhart's Law
"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." If you optimize a skill to sound like someone, you optimize for surface mimicry, not actual competence. The metric (sounds like them) replaces the goal (thinks like them).
